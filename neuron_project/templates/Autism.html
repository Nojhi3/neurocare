Autism, or Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD):

Autism, or Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), is a disorder pertaining to neuron development in brain. Symptoms include challenges in social interaction, communication, and repetitive behaviors.
The exact cause of autism is not known, but it's believed to involve a combination of genetic and environmental factors.

  • Social Challenges:
    - Trouble making friends and understanding emotions.
    - Finds it hard to look others in the eye or share feelings.
  • Communication Difficulties:
    - Talks later than other kids or repeats words (echolalia).
    - Struggles with gestures and tone of voice.
  • Repetitive Behaviors:
    - Does the same actions over and over, like hand-flapping.
    - Doesn't like changes, wants things to stay the same.
  • Sensory Sensitivities:
    - Reacts strongly to lights, sounds, or textures.
    - Likes or dislikes certain sensory experiences.
  • Difficulty with Transitions:
    - Gets upset with unexpected changes or new routines.
  • Fixation on Specific Topics:
    - Really focuses on specific interests, may not switch easily.
    - Talks a lot about particular subjects.
  • Loss of Items: Frequently misplaces homework, books, jackets, backpacks, or sports equipment.

Consult a Doctor: If parents observe a persistent and significant presence of these behaviors, adversely affecting the child's academic, social, and daily life functioning, it is advisable to consult a healthcare professional for a comprehensive assessment and guidance on appropriate management strategies. Early intervention and support can contribute to better outcomes for children with Autism.

Indoor Games For Autism


Match The Card

Memory matching cards are easily available in the market. You can play this simple game by arranging pairs of matching cards face down in random order. You and your child can take turns in flipping the cards. You need to flip twice, and if you get a matching pair, you can take the cards. If not, you continue playing until all the cards are matched.


Age

3 to 5 years

Benefits

Memory, concentration and social skills.

Suitable for

Speech and Language delay, learning disability

Sorting items

Give your child a plate with a variety of snacks that are different in shape, colour and texture. Ask your child to sort the items based on these characteristics. You can also use this sensory activity to teach basic maths skills such as addition and subtraction. Reward your child if he cooperates well with the activity by letting him eat the snack.


Age

3 to 5 years

Benefits

Motor coordination and problem solving

Suitable for

Motor coordination and problem solving

Build structures

Blocks are a staple in every child's toy collection, so dump them out and start playing. Play dough is another method develop fine motor skills. When children are playing with the playdough, rolling, squishing or poking it, they are building strength in the muscles in their fingers and hands. This will help them in holding a pencil, coloring or manipulating items such as scissors, and buttons

Dance to a tune

Dancing is great for self-expression and for keeping your child's body in shape. And all that tapping, stomping and twirling are so much fun. Games like Twister and Just Dance can help.Even for children who are wheelchair-bound, dancing can help in flexibility and improve upper body strength. When you incorporate music into it, your child's brain gets a boost of endorphins, the feel-good hormones, which will relax her. Also, by involving in a dance session with friends and family, your child can hone her social skills.


Age

3 to 5 years, 6 to 9 years

Benefits

Confidence, spatial awareness, motor and social skills

Suitable for

Down Syndrome, Autism Spectrum Disorders, Physical disability, Cerebral Palsy, Learning Disability
